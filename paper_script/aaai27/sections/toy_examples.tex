\section{Randomized Toy Experiments}

The previous section identifies Property X as the availability of close
substitute solutions in a decomposable feasible set. We now test this mechanism
in randomized toy families rather than relying on a single hand-designed
example. The goal is not to prove a theorem about every packing or path
problem, but to separate two effects: how often prediction noise changes the
selected solution identity, and how much true-objective regret that identity
change creates.

\paragraph{Randomized families.}
The generated sweep includes the original abstract decomposable packing family
as a clean control, but the paper-facing comparison focuses on three stochastic
families. The first is a random KEP-like set-packing family: we generate 12
patient-donor pair vertices, random directed compatibility arcs with probability
0.35, enumerate candidate 2- and 3-cycles, and choose a maximum-weight set of
vertex-disjoint cycles. The second is a stable-set realization of the same
close-substitute mechanism: the conflict graph is a disjoint union of 12
cliques with 4 candidate vertices per clique, so an independent set can choose
at most one vertex from each clique. With positive weights, the oracle and
predicted solutions choose one non-conflicting vertex per clique. The third is
a path-like control with 4 parallel $s$--$t$ paths, each containing 12 edges;
selecting a different path changes the entire coupled route.
Figure~\ref{fig:randomized-toy-setup} visualizes these three feasibility
structures; the random true coefficients and prediction noise are generated on
top of these structures.

All families are controlled by two parameters. The parameter $\tau$ controls
the separation between alternatives, and $\sigma$ controls independent
prediction noise. For each $(\tau,\sigma)$ pair, we generate 500 random
instances. For each instance, we compute the oracle solution under true
coefficients, the two-stage solution under noisy predicted coefficients, the
normalized regret of the two-stage solution, whether the solution identity
changes, and the true oracle--second-best gap. We use the two-stage normalized
regret as the instance-level improvement ceiling: no decision-focused method
can improve over two-stage by more than the remaining gap to oracle.

\paragraph{Close substitutes: frequent identity changes, small regret.}
Table~\ref{tab:randomized-toy-summary} reports representative settings from the
randomized sweep. In the close-substitute regime ($\tau=0.02$,
$\sigma=0.10$), the KEP-like set-packing family has $53.8\%$ identity mismatch
but only $0.21\%$ mean normalized regret, with $99.8\%$ of instances within
$5\%$ of oracle. The clustered stable-set family shows an even stronger identity
effect: identity mismatch is $100\%$, but mean regret is only $1.05\%$ and all
instances are within $5\%$ of oracle. Thus, in these close-substitute families,
noisy two-stage predictions often change the selected solution, but the selected
solution is still a close substitute under the true objective.

The shortest-path control shows why the problem label alone is not the full
explanation. In the close-substitute path regime, the path family also has
small mean regret ($0.47\%$), because the alternative paths are themselves
close. This supports the conditional version of our hypothesis: close
substitutes, not the problem label by itself, limit the room for
decision-focused learning.

\paragraph{Separated alternatives: regret can reappear.}
When alternatives are more separated and prediction noise is larger
($\tau=0.30$, $\sigma=0.30$), the close-substitute story becomes conditional.
Mean regret rises to $3.27\%$ for the KEP-like set-packing family and $7.57\%$
for stable set, and the within-$5\%$ rates fall to $73.0\%$ and $24.8\%$,
respectively. This does not contradict Property X; it clarifies it. Vertex- or
block-level substitutes can limit regret, but if prediction noise moves the
solution away from enough good local components, the losses can accumulate and
leave more room for decision-focused learning.

For the path family in the same separated regime, the unconditional mean regret
is $2.30\%$ because identity mismatch occurs in only $39.8\%$ of instances.
Conditional on a mismatch, however, the mean regret is $5.78\%$, almost the
same as the mean oracle--second-best gap of $5.80\%$. This is the path-like
contrast: when a ranking error redirects the route to a materially worse path,
the regret is controlled by the path-level best-second gap.

\paragraph{Takeaway.}
The randomized toy experiments support the conditional Property X hypothesis.
For combinatorial problems with close substitute solutions, two-stage
prediction can select a different solution identity while leaving little
decision regret for DFL to remove. This pattern appears in a random KEP-like
cycle-packing model and in a stable-set formulation with conflict constraints.
Conversely, when the selected alternative is materially worse, as can happen
when many local components are changed or when a path is redirected to a worse
route, the regret and the potential value of decision-focused learning both
increase.
