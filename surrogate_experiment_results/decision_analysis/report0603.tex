\section{Step 1: Implementation Validation with PyEPO Reference}
\label{sec:implementation-validation}

\subsection{Motivation}
The goal of this step is to check whether my implementation contains subtle
bugs. The logic is simple: if my code cannot reproduce PyEPO behavior on a
standard benchmark or on the same KEP oracle, then there may be a bug in the
loss, gradient, optimizer update, solver interface, or metric calculation.

\section{Step 2: Subset-Resampling Variability}
\label{sec:resampling-variability}

\subsection{Motivation}
The second step is to turn a single-seed observation into distributional
evidence. The current resampling experiment intentionally isolates one source
of randomness: the training subset. The graph pool, label regime, validation
set, heldout set, and training initialization are fixed. Only the subset seed
used to sample 50 training graphs is varied.

Thus, this experiment should be interpreted as:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Train-subset resampling on a fixed graph pool and fixed label regime.}
\end{quote}

It does not yet test graph-generation seed, label-noise seed, or training
initialization seed.

\section{Step 3: Decision-Level Explanation}
\label{sec:decision-analysis}

\subsection{Research Question and Hypothesis}
After completing the implementation validation and subset-resampling
experiments, the next question is no longer whether the code is correct, but
why the observed decision behavior occurs. In particular, the advisor's
hypothesis was:

\begin{quote}
\emph{The two-stage model may remain competitive because bad edge-level
predictions do not necessarily affect the final KEP solution.}
\end{quote}

Therefore, the goal of this section is to move from aggregate test gaps to
decision-level evidence. The main questions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item When 2stage and SPO+ have similar decision quality, are they selecting
    the same KEP solution?
    \item If they select different solutions, are these solutions near-optimal
    alternatives under the true objective?
    \item Are large edge-level prediction errors actually decision-critical, or
    do they often occur on edges that do not affect the selected solution?
    \item When SPO+ improves over 2stage, does it reduce errors on
    decision-changing edges?
\end{enumerate}

The central distinction is between \emph{raw prediction error} and
\emph{decision-critical prediction error}. A large edge prediction error matters
only if it changes the selected KEP solution or appears in the symmetric
difference between the predicted and oracle solutions.

\subsection{Analysis Scope}
This first-pass analysis focuses on the most difficult noiseless polynomial
misspecification setting:

\[
\texttt{Step2b d8}, \qquad n_{\mathrm{train}} = 50,
\qquad \texttt{evaluation} = \texttt{heldout400}.
\]

The two compared methods are:

\[
\text{2stage selected by validation MSE}
\qquad \text{vs.} \qquad
\text{SPO+ selected by validation SPO+ loss}.
\]

This is intentionally a narrow setting. The goal is not to claim a universal
mechanism across all regimes, but to obtain a clean first-pass explanation in a
high-misspecification setting where 2stage and SPO+ differences are meaningful.

\subsection{Analysis Design}
The decision-level analysis has four stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Seed selection.} Select representative subset seeds from the
    resampling results: large SPO+ improvement, weak/borderline improvement,
    and easy low-gap cases.

    \item \textbf{Per-graph decision replay.} For each selected seed and each
    heldout graph, replay the 2stage and SPO+ checkpoints and record the
    selected KEP solutions.

    \item \textbf{Edge-level criticality analysis.} For each graph, compute
    edge-level prediction errors and classify each edge according to whether it
    appears in the oracle solution, predicted solution, or their symmetric
    difference.

    \item \textbf{Mechanism extraction.} Use summary plots, correlation
    analysis, near-tie analysis, and case studies to identify why 2stage
    sometimes remains competitive and when SPO+ helps.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Seed Selection: Why These 9 Seeds}
The selected seeds were chosen from the subset-resampling results, not manually
from individual graphs. The idea is to cover three qualitatively different
situations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Large improvement:} 2stage has relatively high decision gap,
    while SPO+ substantially improves over it.
    \item \textbf{Weak or borderline improvement:} SPO+ does not strongly
    improve over 2stage.
    \item \textbf{Easy low-gap:} both 2stage and SPO+ have low decision gaps.
\end{itemize}

The selected seeds are:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{large improvement:} &\quad \{1,25,22\},\\
\text{weak/borderline improvement:} &\quad \{27,21,30\},\\
\text{easy low-gap:} &\quad \{41,16,33\}.
\end{aligned}
\]

This selection makes the later case studies interpretable: we can compare
cases where SPO+ clearly helps, cases where both methods are already good, and
cases where the improvement is weak.

\subsection{Decision Replay: From Aggregate Gap to Per-Graph Decisions}
Aggregate heldout400 gaps are useful, but they do not reveal what decision was
made on each graph. Therefore, for each selected seed, I replayed the trained
2stage and SPO+ checkpoints on every heldout graph and recorded:

\begin{itemize}
    \item the oracle solution $y^\star$ under the true reward
    $w_{\mathrm{true}}$;
    \item the 2stage-selected solution $y_{\mathrm{2stage}}$;
    \item the SPO+-selected solution $y_{\mathrm{SPO+}}$;
    \item true objective values and decision gaps;
    \item solution overlap metrics such as Jaccard similarity with the oracle
    solution.
\end{itemize}

For a method $m$, the decision gap is:

\[
\mathrm{gap}_m
=
w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y^\star
-
w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y_m,
\]

and the normalized gap is:

\[
\mathrm{normalized\ gap}_m
=
\frac{
w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y^\star
-
w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y_m
}{
\left|w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y^\star\right| + \epsilon
}.
\]

To ensure that this replay follows the same evaluation path as the original
Step2 heldout400 metrics, the replayed per-graph gaps were checked against each
seed's existing \path{metrics/test_per_graph.csv}. The maximum differences
were:

\[
\max |\Delta \mathrm{gap}| = 5.07\times 10^{-5}, \qquad
\max |\Delta \mathrm{normalized\ gap}| = 1.65\times 10^{-7}.
\]

These differences are within the intended tolerance, so the following analysis
is based on the same decision path as the existing Step2 evaluation.

\subsection{Edge Criticality: From Raw Error to Decision-Changing Error}
The advisor's hypothesis is about edge-level prediction errors. For each edge
$e$, I computed:

\[
\mathrm{err}_e^{(m)} =
\left|
\hat{w}_e^{(m)} - w_{\mathrm{true},e}
\right|,
\]

where $m$ is either 2stage or SPO+. Each edge is then classified using the
following binary indicators:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Indicator & Meaning \\
\midrule
\texttt{in\_opt} & edge appears in the oracle solution $y^\star$ \\
\texttt{in\_2stage} & edge appears in $y_{\mathrm{2stage}}$ \\
\texttt{in\_spoplus} & edge appears in $y_{\mathrm{SPO+}}$ \\
\texttt{in\_2stage\_symdiff} & edge appears in $y^\star \triangle y_{\mathrm{2stage}}$ \\
\texttt{in\_spoplus\_symdiff} & edge appears in $y^\star \triangle y_{\mathrm{SPO+}}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The symmetric difference is the key object:

\[
y^\star \triangle y_m
=
\{e: y^\star_e \neq y_{m,e}\}.
\]

Edges in this set are precisely the edges where the predicted solution differs
from the oracle solution. Thus, prediction errors on these edges are more
likely to be decision-critical than prediction errors on edges that are never
selected.

There are three main observations.

First, SPO+ has a lower mean normalized decision gap and higher average solution
overlap with the oracle solution. This confirms that SPO+ is not only improving
a numerical loss but is also selecting edge sets that are closer to the oracle
solution.

Second, the top-10-error symmetric-difference rate is relatively low. For
2stage, only 9.39\% of top-10-error edges enter the symmetric difference with

large edge-level prediction errors are not decision-changing.

Third, all-edge MSE is weakly correlated with decision gap. In contrast, error
measures restricted to symmetric-difference edges are much more predictive.
This suggests that raw prediction accuracy is not enough to explain decision
quality; what matters is whether errors occur on decision-changing edges.

\subsection{Best-vs-Second Check for the Selected Cases}
The previous case studies compare the model-selected rank-1 solution against
the oracle solution. To check whether these examples also support the
near-optimal-alternative explanation, I computed the best and second-best
distinct edge-selection solutions under each model's predicted weights.

There are two different margins here and they should not be mixed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item the \textbf{oracle gap}
    $w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y^\star - w_{\mathrm{true}}^\top y$, which says
    how far a rank-1 or rank-2 solution is from the true oracle;
    \item the \textbf{predicted best-second margin}
    $\hat w^\top y_{\mathrm{rank1}}-\hat w^\top y_{\mathrm{rank2}}$, which
    says how much the model itself prefers rank 1 over rank 2.
\end{itemize}

The main quantity for the story is the oracle gap. The predicted margin is
only an auxiliary diagnostic: a small predicted margin means the model itself
sees the top two solutions as close.

The selected cases now separate into the intended mechanisms. In Case A, rank
1 is exactly oracle for both methods. Rank 2 is often still near-oracle, but
not always; either way, the large edge prediction errors did not change the
top decision. In Case B, rank 1 is not oracle but has near-zero gap, and all

evidence for a flat near-optimal plateau. In Case C, 2stage rank 1 is bad in
all three examples, but rank 2 distinguishes failure modes: \texttt{G-1560}

whereas \texttt{G-392} and \texttt{G-39} keep high 2stage rank-2 gaps,
suggesting that the top predicted region is also poor. SPO+ moves rank 1 back
toward the near-oracle region, but its rank 2 can be much worse, showing that
top-1 ordering matters in some decision-critical cases.

\subsection{Answer to the Advisor's Hypothesis and Limitations}
The first-pass evidence supports the hypothesis. The reason 2stage can remain
competitive is not that it predicts every edge accurately. Instead, many large
edge-level errors are non-critical because they occur on edges that are not
selected or do not enter the symmetric difference. In addition, KEP graphs may
contain near-optimal alternative solutions, so two different selected edge sets
can have very similar true objectives.

SPO+ appears most helpful when the 2stage prediction errors fall on
decision-changing edges. In those cases, SPO+ reduces both the decision gap and
the involvement of high-error edges in the symmetric difference.

This is still a first-pass analysis. The current limitations are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item only train size 50 and heldout400 are used;
    \item only 9 representative subset seeds are selected;
    \item \texttt{Step2c} multiplicative-noise regimes are not yet analyzed;
    \item the near-tie analysis uses only observed candidates
    $y^\star$, $y_{\mathrm{2stage}}$, and $y_{\mathrm{SPO+}}$;
    \item the best-vs-second analysis only inspects the top two predicted
    distinct edge-selection solutions, not the full feasible solution set;
    \item adversarial SPO+ solutions $y_{\mathrm{adv}}$ are not yet included.
\end{itemize}

The next step is to extend this same analysis to \texttt{Step2c d8 eps050}, add
FY, and include $y_{\mathrm{adv}}$ to inspect how SPO+ gradients push the
predictions away from bad decision-changing solutions.
